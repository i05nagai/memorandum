## 2.2 Fundamental theory

### 2.2.1 Basic concpets

#### Definition 6 log loss function
The average loss functon

$$
\begin{equation}
    L(w)
    :=
    -\int_{\mathcal{R}(X)}
        \log p(x \mid w)

    \label{equation_02_07}
\end{equation}
$$

THe emperical loss funnciton are defined as

$$
\begin{equation}
    L_{n}(w)

    -

    .
    \label{equation_02_08}
\end{equation}
$$

$$

### 2.2.2 Normized observables


We recall that

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
    w_{0} \in W,
    \
    f(x, w)
    & := &
        \log
        \frac{
            p(x \mid w_{0})
        }{
            p(x \mid w)
        }
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        \log
        \frac{
            q(x)
        }{
            p(x \mid w)
        }
    \nonumber
\end{eqnarray}
    .
$$

With this notation, we can 

$$
\begin{equation}
    p(x \mid w)
    =
    p(x \mid w_{0})
    e^{-f(x, w)}
    \label{equaiton_relation_non_logarithmic_likehood}
\end{equation}
    .
$$

#### Definiiton 7
* $q(x)$,
    * true distribution
* $p(x \mid w)$,

The normalized average log loss funciton are defined as

$$
\begin{equation}
    K(w)
    :=
        \int_{\mathcal{R}(X)}
            f(x, w)
        \ P_{X}(dx)
    \nonumber
\end{equation}
    .
$$

The normalized empirical log loss funcgtions are defined as

$$
\begin{equation}
    K_{n}(w)
    :=
        \frac{1}{n}
        \sum_{i=1}^{n}
            f(X_{i}, w)
    \nonumber
\end{equation}
    .
$$

<div class="end-of-statement" style="text-align: right">■</div>

The immediate consequence of the definiton of the normalized logs loss functions is

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
    L(w)
    & = &
        \int_{\mathcal{R}(X)}
            -
            \log p(x \mid w)
            +
            \log p(x \mid w_{0})
            -
            \log p(x \mid w_{0})
        \ P_{X}(dx)
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        \int_{\mathcal{R}(X)}
            f(x, w)
            -
            \log p(x \mid w_{0})
        \ P_{X}(dx)
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        L(w_{0})
        +
        K(w)
    \nonumber
    \\
    L_{n}(w)
    & = &
        \frac{1}{n}
        \sum_{i=1}^{n}
        \left(
            -
            \log p(X_{i} \mid w)
            +
            \log p(X_{i} \mid w_{0})
            -
            \log p(X_{i} \mid w_{0})
        \right)
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        \frac{1}{n}
        \sum_{i=1}^{n}
        \left(
            f(X_{i}, w)
            -
            \log p(X_{i} \mid w_{0})
        \right)
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        L_{n}(w_{0})
        +
        K_{n}(w)
    .
    \nonumber
\end{eqnarray}
$$

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
    -K(w)
    & = &
        -
        \mathrm{E}
        \left[
            \log
                \frac{
                    q(X)
                }{
                    p(X \mid w)
                }
        \right]
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        \mathrm{E}
        \left[
            \log
                \frac{
                    p(X \mid w)
                }{
                    q(X)
                }
        \right]
    \nonumber
    \\
    & \le &
        \log
            \mathrm{E}
            \left[
                \frac{
                    p(X \mid w)
                }{
                    q(X)
                }
            \right]
        \quad
        (\because \text{Jensen's inequality})
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        \log
        \left(
            \int_{\mathcal{R}(X)}
                p(X \mid w)
            \ dx
        \right)
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        0
    \nonumber
\end{eqnarray}
$$

Hence $K(w) \ge 0$.
Moreover,

$$
    K(w)
    =
    0
    \Leftrightarrow
    w \in W_{0}
    .
$$

Indeed, it is easy to confirm

$$
    w \in W_{0}
    \Rightarrow
    K(w)
    =
    0
    .
$$

Suppose that

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
    P(
        \log q(X)
        \neq
        \log p(X \mid x)
    )
    & = &
        P(
            \log q(X)
            >
            \log p(X \mid x)
        )
        +
        P(
            \log q(X)
            <
            \log p(X \mid x)
        )
    \nonumber
    \\
    & > &
        0
    \nonumber
\end{eqnarray}
    .
$$

If $P( \log q(X) > \log p(X \mid x))$,

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
    \mathrm{E}
    \left[
        1_{\{\log q(X) > \log p(X \mid w) \}}
        \log
            \frac{
                q(x)
            }{
                p(x \mid w)
            }
        +
        1_{\{\log q(X) < \log p(X \mid w) \}}
        \log
            \frac{
                q(x)
            }{
                p(x \mid w)
            }
    \right]
    & \ge &
        \mathrm{E}
        \left[
            1_{\{\log q(X) > \log p(X \mid w) \}}
            \log
                \frac{
                    q(x)
                }{
                    p(x \mid w)
                }
        \right]
    \nonumber
    \\
    & > &
        0
    \nonumber
\end{eqnarray}
$$

On the other hand, If $P( \log q(X) < \log p(X \mid x))$,

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
    \mathrm{E}
    \left[
        1_{\{\log q(X) > \log p(X \mid w) \}}
        \log
            \frac{
                q(x)
            }{
                p(x \mid w)
            }
        +
        1_{\{\log q(X) < \log p(X \mid w) \}}
        \log
            \frac{
                q(x)
            }{
                p(x \mid w)
            }
    \right]
    & \ge &
        \mathrm{E}
        \left[
            1_{\{\log q(X) < \log p(X \mid w) \}}
            \log
                \frac{
                    q(x)
                }{
                    p(x \mid w)
                }
        \right]
    \nonumber
    \\
    & < &

    .
    \nonumber
\end{eqnarray}
$$

In both cases, the inequalities violates the assumption.

#### Definition the normalized marginal likelihood


$$

$$
\begin{eqnarray}

    \nonumber
    \\

        \int_{W}
            \phi(w)
            \prod_{i=1}^{n}
                p(X_{i} \mid w_{0})^{\beta}
                \exp
                \left(
                    -
                    \sum_{j=1}^{n}
                        f(X_{j}, w)
                \right)
        \ dw
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        \int_{W}
            \phi(w)
            \prod_{i=1}^{n}
                p(X_{i} \mid w_{0})^{\beta}
                \exp
                \left(
                    -
                    n
                    K_{n}(w)
                \right)
        \ dw

    & = &
        Z_{n}^{(0)}(\beta)
        \prod_{i=1}^{n}

    .
    \nonumber
\end{eqnarray}
$$

Posterior distribution can be written

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
    p(w \mid X^{n})
    & = &
        \frac{
            1
        }{
            Z_{n}(X^{n}; \beta)
        }
        \phi(w)
        \prod_{i=1}^{n}

    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        \frac{
            1
        }{
            Z_{n}^{(0)}(\beta)
            \prod_{i=1}^{n}
                p(X_{i} \mid w_{0})^{\beta}

        \phi(w)
        \prod_{i=1}^{n}
            p(X_{i} \mid w)^{\beta}
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        \frac{
            \phi(w)
        }{
            Z_{n}^{(0)}(\beta)
        }
        \prod_{i=1}^{n}
            \frac{
                p(X_{i} \mid w)^{\beta}
            }{
                p(X_{i} \mid w_{0})^{\beta}
            }
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        \frac{
            \phi(w)
        }{
            Z_{n}^{(0)}(\beta)
        }

            \exp
            \left(
                \log
                    \frac{
                        p(X_{i} \mid w)^{\beta}
                    }{

                    }
            \right)
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        \frac{
            \phi(w)
        }{
            Z_{n}^{(0)}(\beta)
        }
        \exp
        \left(
            -
            \sum_{i=1}^{n}
            \log
                \frac{
                    p(X_{i} \mid w_{0})^{\beta}

                }

    \nonumber
    \\

        \right)

$$

#### Remark 16
In a later chapter, we will show if the log density ratio function $f(w, x)$ is relatively finite variance, there exist $\lambda, n > 0$ such that 

$$
    \hat{Z}_{n}^{(0)}
    :=
    \frac{
        n^{\lambda}
    }{
        (\log n)^{m-1}
    }
    Z_{n}^{(0)}(\beta)
$$

converges in distribution as $n \rightarrow \infty$.
If posterior distribution can be approximated by a normal distribution, $\lambda = d/2$ and $m = 1$.
On the other hand, since the normalized marginal likihood is written as

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
    Z_{n}^{(0)}((X_{i}); 1)
    & := &
        \int_{W}
            \prod_{i=1}^{n}
                \frac{
                    p(X_{i} \mid w)
                }{
                    q(X_{i})
                }
            \phi(w)
        \ dw
\end{eqnarray}
    ,
$$

for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$ we have

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
    \int_{\mathcal{R}(X_{1})}
    \cdots
    \int_{\mathcal{R}(X_{n})}
        Z_{n}^{(0)}((x_{i}); 1)
    \ P_{X_{1}}(d x_{1})
    \cdots
    \ P_{X_{n}}(d x_{n})
    & = &
        \int_{\mathcal{R}(X_{1})}
        \cdots
        \int_{\mathcal{R}(X_{n})}
            Z_{n}^{(0)}((x_{i}); 1)
        \ P_{X_{1}}(d x_{1})
        \cdots
        \ P_{X_{n}}(d x_{n})
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        \int_{\mathcal{R}(X_{1})}
        \cdots
        \int_{\mathcal{R}(X_{n})}
            \int_{W}
                \prod_{i=1}^{n}
                    \frac{
                        p(x_{i} \mid w)
                    }{
                        q(x_{i})
                    }
                \phi(w)
            \ dw
            q(x_{1})
            \cdots
            q(x_{n})
        \ P_{X_{1}}(d x_{1})
        \cdots
        \ P_{X_{n}}(d x_{n})
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        \int_{\mathcal{R}(X_{1})}

        \cdots

                    p_{X_{i}, \Theta}(x_{i}, w)
            \ dw
        \ P_{X_{1}}(d x_{1})
        \cdots
        \ P_{X_{n}}(d x_{n})
    \nonumber
    \\
    & = &
        1
    \nonumber
\end{eqnarray}
$$

where $\Theta: \Omega \rightarrow W$ is r.v. which takes value in parameter space $W$.
Hence

$$
\begin{eqnarray}

        \quad
        (n \rightarrow \infty)
    .
    \nonumber
\end{eqnarray}
$$

Therefore, $$\hat{Z}_{n}^{(0)}((x_{i}); 1)$$ converges in distribution, however, its expecation does not converge.
